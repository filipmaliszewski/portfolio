FILIP MALISZEWSKI

In November 2024 I came across a news story about a young German man named Jascha Hardenberg, who vanished without a trace on the remote island of Madeira, Portugal. He had gone missing during a hike in the Rabaçal area on December 29, 2020, just one day after posting a photo of himself at a local Christmas market. That Facebook image was the last known trace of him. Madeira caught my attention for more than just its mystery. It’s a small, isolated island with limited escape routes, and yet multiple people disappear there each year without a body ever being found. In the 21st century, in a world where fridges can text you about running out of almond milk because they know you’re near a supermarket, how is it possible for someone to vanish without leaving a digital trace? I launched my own amateur investigation, blending art with obsession. I found a publicly accessible webcam streaming live from the same Christmas market where Jascha had taken his last photo. I wrote an AI-powered facial recognition bot using MTCNN to scan and save the faces of people passing through the footage. In 7 minutes, I collected over 80,000 face-shots - portraits of unsuspecting individuals captured by a camera they never noticed. This project confronts a strange paradox: the same surveillance infrastructure that commodifies us for profit fails us when it comes to real human need. We live in a world saturated with data, yet when a person disappears, that data often proves elusive. Here I was, a 23-year-old art student, casually scraping faces from livestreams in my slightly grimy Nieuw-West apartment, and it was disturbingly easy. It made me question not just the ethics of surveillance, but who it really serves.
